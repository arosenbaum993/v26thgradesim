Source 1: "How Artificial Intelligence Works"
Informational Text

Artificial intelligence — often shortened to AI — refers to computer systems that can perform tasks that normally require human thinking. These tasks include recognizing speech, identifying images, translating languages, and making decisions based on patterns in data.

At the heart of most modern AI is a process called machine learning. Instead of being programmed with every rule it needs to follow, a machine-learning system is trained on large amounts of data. For example, to teach an AI to recognize photos of cats, engineers feed it thousands of images labeled "cat" and "not a cat." Over time, the system learns to detect patterns — such as pointy ears, whiskers, and fur — and applies those patterns to new images it has never seen before.

A more advanced form of machine learning, called deep learning, uses layers of interconnected mathematical models called neural networks. These networks are loosely inspired by the human brain, which processes information through billions of connected nerve cells. Deep learning has made it possible for AI systems to perform remarkably complex tasks, such as diagnosing diseases from medical scans, generating realistic images, and holding conversations in natural language.

AI is already part of everyday life in ways many people don't notice. When a streaming service recommends a show you might like, that is AI analyzing your viewing habits. When a navigation app reroutes your drive around traffic, that is AI processing real-time data. Spam filters, voice assistants, and even the autocorrect on your phone all rely on forms of artificial intelligence.

Despite its rapid growth, AI has limitations. It can reflect the biases present in its training data, produce errors when faced with situations far outside its training, and raise serious questions about privacy, jobs, and the ethical use of technology. Scientists, engineers, policymakers, and the public are all working to understand both the promise and the risks of this powerful technology.

Source 2: "The Space Race and the Technology It Left Behind"
Informational Text

In the 1950s and 1960s, the United States and the Soviet Union were locked in a fierce competition known as the Space Race. Each nation poured enormous resources into developing rockets, satellites, and spacecraft, driven partly by national pride and partly by the belief that whoever controlled space would gain a strategic advantage in the Cold War.

The race produced remarkable milestones. In 1957, the Soviet Union launched Sputnik, the world's first artificial satellite. Four years later, cosmonaut Yuri Gagarin became the first human to orbit Earth. The United States responded with its own program, culminating in the Apollo 11 mission of 1969, when astronauts Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin became the first humans to walk on the Moon.

What is less well known is how profoundly the Space Race transformed everyday life. Engineers working on NASA projects needed to solve difficult problems — how to build equipment small enough to fit inside a spacecraft, how to keep astronauts safe from extreme temperatures and radiation, and how to communicate across vast distances in space. The solutions they invented spilled over into civilian technology in unexpected ways.

Memory foam, originally developed to protect pilots and astronauts from high-speed impacts, is now found in mattresses and sports helmets. Water filtration technology created for long space missions is used today in portable water purifiers. Scratch-resistant lenses in eyeglasses were adapted from coatings developed for NASA helmet visors. Even the technology behind wireless headsets, GPS navigation, and certain medical imaging tools traces its roots to the space program.

Today, space exploration continues — through Mars rovers, the James Webb Space Telescope, and growing commercial spaceflight industries. Scientists argue that the next wave of space research will again push the limits of engineering, materials science, and computing, generating innovations we cannot yet predict. As it was in the 1960s, the challenges of going beyond Earth continue to drive technology forward here at home.
